I. Kingdom of Benin

:|| Once upon a time in West Africa, a powerful kingdom emerged known as the Kingdom of Benin. This civilization thrived from the 11th century to 1897 A.D., leaving behind a legacy of remarkable achievements..
Known for their mastery in bronze sculpting, the Benin people created intricate Benin bronzes that captivated the world with their beauty and craftsmanship.

Our People

The San peoples (also Saan), or Bushmen, are the members of the indigenous hunter-gatherer cultures of southern Africa, and the oldest surviving cultures of the region. Their ancestral territories span Botswana, Namibia, Angola, Zambia, Zimbabwe, Lesotho and South Africa.

San Peoples

They speak, or their ancestors spoke, languages of the Khoe, Tuu and Kxʼa language families, and are only a 'people' in contrast to pastoralists such as the Khoekhoe and descendants of more recent waves of immigration such as the Bantu, Europeans and Asians. In 2017, Botswana was home to approximately 63,500 San, making it the country with the highest proportion of San people at 2.8%

Conculsion

The ancient civilizations of Africa left a lasting legacy with their remarkable achievements in various fields. From the artistic heritage of Benin to the grandeur of Egypt's pyramids and the cultural exchange between Axum and the Sabaeans, these civilizations played a vital role in shaping the history of the African continent. The kingdoms of Sudan, including Kerma, Kush, Nobatia, Makuria, and Alodia, added to the richness and diversity of Africa's ancient past. Exploring and celebrating these civilizations inspire a deeper understanding and appreciation of Africa's historical and cultural heritage.

In the golden age of ancient Egypt, known as the New Kingdom, the civilization reached its pinnacle of power. The pharaohs expanded their rule, dominating Nubia and parts of the Near East. With wealth flowing into the kingdom, Egypt flourished culturally, economically, and politically.
